Elementary — High School

Building discipline

I have been playing the cello for as long as I can remember. My parents are both professional musicians (cellist & pianist), and when our whole family moved back to Korea, I slipped into the Korean classical music pipeline like it was the most natural thing in the world.

By 5th grade, I was practicing around 4 hours a day, and by 6th grade, I was excused from school for months to spend 9+ hours at the practice studio to prepare for the music school entrance exams. I got in and spent about two more years at an institution where I built the deepest foundation of discipline in my life.

The school was the most competitive music school at the time, and they picked 15 cellists a year through blind auditions where everyone plays the same 2 pieces announced 6 months prior. The semester was built around the performance exams that would come at the end, graded by judges sitting behind a curtain. The grades would determine your ranking, which was revealed when you sat in orchestra in that same order. There were fundamental exams where you were tested on scales and etudes, required to retake until perfected. Music theory exams were twice a semester, and then was the development concert in front of the whole grade. Midterms and Finals consisted of 2 weeks of cramming with little sleep, because typically practicing cello took priority. Many competed outside of school too, which was basically a miniature repeat of school competition.

I pulled an all-nighter for the first time when I was 13, and throughout middle school I was micro-managing my day down to the minute, running home after school to put in the hours that my peers would most certainly be putting in as well.

I learned to keep my seat warm. Korean people say "엉덩이 붙여," which means stick your butt to the seat. I learned during this time how to sit and put in the sheer hours, put in the work even when I was falling asleep, even when I had a fever, and even on holidays and birthdays.

When I moved to an international school that wasn't specialized in music, it was hard because academics were much harder, I was tested in a new language, and I still had to fill in the hours of practice I had set for myself. For a month I vowed to practice 10 hours a day because I felt like I was falling behind, and I did it even if it meant I slept at 5am.

Music is no longer what I want to do professionally. But I really value this time in my life because I believe I was exposed to learn discipline in a way that not many teenagers get to experience, and it has built the foundation for the drive and consistency I practice now.
